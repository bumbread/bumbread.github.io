    Overview of PC history #

    Before we dive into inspecting various components that participate in boot-time function of the OS, I would like to make a special overview of the history of PCs. This will make the understanding of BIOS as well as other “features” of the booting process much easier.

    The term PC was due to one mainstream computer from IBM called the IBM PC. Nowadays the term “PC” most of the time refers to an x86 computer capable of running Windows, Linux. PC can be contrasted to another term – “Mac”.

    1. IBM PC #

    IBM PC was using the Intel 8088 processor, which is a cost-reduced variant of Intel 8086 processor.

    Intel 8086 was a 16-bit processor, which could store numbers up to FFFFh (65535). The address bus was 20 bits, which meant processor could address up to 1 Mb of memory.

    The first 640 Kb of the 1 Mb address space was given to OS and user programs, the other 384 Kb were dedicated to video memory, and the motherboard ROM.

    The main storage media at the time were the floppy drives and hard drives. Their physical structure was similar. Each one of them was split into 512-byte sectors. It is only possible to read or write the whole sector at once (no single-byte writes). The difference between floppies and hard drives is that floppies were used as portable media just like USB flash is used now.

    BIOS in IBM PCs was the hardware abstraction layer that allowed OSes to perform hardware input/output functions independently of the actual hardware used in the system.

    2. IBM PC Compatibles #

    It was desirable for many companies to produce and sell similar and compatible to IBM PC computers. Those computers are called IBM PC Compatibles. Nowadays all PCs that run windows are PC Compatible.

    Companies that sold IBM PC at the time reverse-engeneered IBM PC. Later some parts were standartised, and other remained de-facto standard. Those computers use the same address bus and BIOS with similar API.

    Though many of those systems weren’t fully compatible. It could be that some PC Compatibles were implementing extra functionality, or underimplementing some functionality. Some functionality could be implemented with bugs et cetera.

    3. Now #

    PCs have evolved, some parts of the original IBM PC were standartised and/or replaced (about that later). To this day many computers support legacy interfaces for backwards compatibility reasons.

    The Intel CPUs have evolved. 8086 had evolved into 80186, then into 80286 and then into 80386. The names of the processors all ended in “86”, so collectively they make the x86 processor architecture.

    It was desirable for Intel to keep the backwards compatibility within their processors, so that newer processor could be able to work with older software. That’s why even new 64-bit Intel processors start up in so-called real mode, a special 16-bit mode that uses 20-bit address bus.

    Backwards compatibility is one of the main reasons why learning about booting process feels like visiting an old computers museum :)
